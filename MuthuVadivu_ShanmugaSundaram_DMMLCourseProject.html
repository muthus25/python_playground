Covid-19 and Partisanship of States in the United States¶

Abstract:¶

The response to COVID-19 in the United States has mostly been political. A high level of political polarization is observed among leaders and public. Unlike many other countries, different states in the United States follow different levels of restrictions and do not have the same set of rules. The promptness of action and circumstances of states vary based on the Governors. Also there is an uneven public response to the pandemic across states. Actions of people, on following orders and restrictions put in place, are consistent with the party they follow or believe in. In this paper, we study if there is a significant difference in covid impact, in terms of cases per capita, based on political partisanship of states. More precisely, we research if there is a difference in covid cases per 100k between the states in which Republican and Democratic Governors rule and if there is a significant difference of the same between the red and blue states. We present data on cases per 100k across 50 states and District of Columbia with the party to which each state Governor belongs to and the partisanship of people in the state based on the last 4 presidential elections. On analyzing, we find evidence that there is no significant difference in the means of 'covid cases per 100k' between Republican and Democratic states or between Red and Blue states.

Introduction:¶

The number of coronavirus cases surge across the globe, with more than 19 million reported cases in 188 countries and 700,000 fatalities. The United States is one of the worst-hit countries with at least 4,688,000 cases and 152,000 fatalities. Governors across the country are imposing or lifting restrictions for their state residents and there are substantial variations in how the pandemic is handled in each state. American governors thus play an immense role in making life and death decisions during the time of this outbreak.

There are increased concerns and questions on the implications of the political partisanship of states on the pandemic and the consequences this could lead to. There is no doubt about the partisan diconnect in the response to this virus but with all the speculations, it is important to analyze whether this disparity has anything to do with the coronavirus impact in the states.

One other factor to consider is the public attitude towards this pandemic. Public response and behavior are influenced by the political elites. There is a widened gap between how the republicans and the democrats are concerned about the spread of the virus. A study published by Pew Research Center in the month of June stated that the differences between Republicans' and Democrats' levels of comfort far exceeds other demographic and geographic differences.

Therefore, it is imperative to evaluate if there is a correlation between covid cases and the partisanship of the governor of the state. We will also analyze if there is a correlation between covid cases and the political partisanship of the people in the state(conservatives or liberals). Red states are states that were carried by the Republicans in the last 3-4 presidential elections and Blue states are states that were carried by Democrats in the last 3-4 presidential elections. For convenience, we will ignore the three swing states where each party won twice in the last four presidential elections.

For this research, we will consider the covid cases per capita (covid cases per 100k) as population varies across states and the number of covid cases will not be the right indicator to measure the impact. Therefore, below are the two research questions for which we will find answers as part of this research.

  1. Is there a mean difference in covid cases per capita between states ruled by Republican and Democratic Governors?
  2. Is there a mean difference in covid cases per capita between Red and Blue States?

One might wonder why we must be testing both the questions, assuming that Red States will have Republican Governors and Blue States will have Democratic Governors. But this is not always the case. For example, Kentucky is a red state with a Democratic Governor. Similarly there are few other places where the partisanship of the state is different from that of the Governor. Hence, there is a need to verify both the cases.

#To retrieve data from Wikipedia on the party to which each state Governor belongs to
url = 'https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_current_United_States_governors'

response = requests.get(url)
content = response.content

soup = BeautifulSoup(content, 'lxml')

#Get all table data
td_list = soup.findAll('td')

#Remove junk characters
char_to_remove = "'\n"
pattern = "["+char_to_remove+"]"

States = []
Gov = []

#To fetch the state names and the party to which the Governor belongs, remove junk characters and assign to corresponding lists 
for i in range(-10, 549, 10):
    States.append(re.sub(pattern, "", td_list[i+10].text))
    
for j in range(-6, 554, 10):
    Gov_party = re.sub(pattern, "", td_list[j+10].text)
    if Gov_party[0:10] == 'New Progre':
        Gov_party = Gov_party.replace('New Progre','Republican')
    Gov.append(Gov_party[0:10])
    
#Create a dataframe and add the list of values retrieved above to the dataframe

df_Governors['Governor'] = Gov

Statistical Analysis:¶

We can understand the differences enough from the visualizations but we will still go one step further and perform some Statistical Analysis, considering these data to be samples, as the count reported could vary from the actual data and in addition to that, the count on cases keeps changing every day and the data we have is already 2 weeks old.

We will now perform two sample t-tests on the following research questions,

  1. Is there a mean difference in covid cases (dependent variable) per capita between states ruled by Republican and Democratic Governors(independent variables)?
  2. Is there a mean difference in covid cases (dependent variable) per capita between Red and Blue States(independent variables)?

In order to do this, we will have to test the following assumptions, at first.

  1. Assumption of normality of the dependent variables
  2. Assumption of homogeneity of variance

For testing the first assumption, we will make use of Shapiro-Wilk test and for the second assumption, we will make use Levene's test

#Test the assumption of normality of the dependent variable using Shapiro-Wilk test
def normality_assumption(cases):
    from scipy.stats import shapiro
    stat, p = shapiro(cases)
    print('Shapiro p=%.3f'%p)
    if p>0.05:
        print('Therefore data is drawn from a Gaussian distribution')
    else:
        print('Therefore data is not drawn from a Gaussian distribution')
        
#Test the assumption of homogeneity of variance using Levene's test
def equalvar_assumption(cases1, cases2):
    from scipy.stats import levene
    stat, pvalue = levene(cases1, cases2)
    print('Levene p=%.3f'%pvalue)
    if pvalue>0.05:
        print('Therefore the two groups have equal variances')
    else:
        print('Therefore the two groups do not have equal variances')
        
normality_assumption(cases_repubStates)
normality_assumption(cases_demoStates)
equalvar_assumption(cases_demoStates, cases_repubStates)

#Function to perform two independent sample t-test
def ind_sample_ttest(array1, array2, alpha):
    tstat,p = ttest_ind(array1,array2)
    print('t Test Statistic=%.3f, p=%.3f' % (tstat,p))
    if p > alpha:
        print('No significant difference. Fail to reject H0')
    else:
        print('Significant difference. Reject H0')

#Function call with required parameters
ind_sample_ttest(cases_demoStates,cases_repubStates,0.05)

t Test Statistic=-0.300, p=0.766
No significant difference. Fail to reject H0

Since the p value is more than the alpha value of .05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis and state that the means of covid cases per capita were not statistically significantly different for states with Rupublican and Democratic Governors.

Results:¶

In this paper, we studied difference in covid cases per capita between states ruled by Republican and Democratic Governors and also between Red and Blue states(conservative and liberal states). From the visualizations, we were able to discern that the difference was not significant for both the cases. We also conducted independent sample t test. The assumption of normality was tested and satisfied for the distributional shape of the dependent variable (cases per 100k) for states with Republican Governors and states with Democratic Governors and for red and blue states. Review of the Shapiro-Wilk test for normality suggested that the normality of dependent variables was a reasonable assumption. According to Levene's test, the homogeneity of variance assumption was met. As there was no random assignment to states, the assumption of independence was not met and this creates a potential for increased probability of Type 1 and Type 2 error.

The independent t test indicated that the covid 'cases per capita' means were not statistically significantly different for states ruled by Republican Governors and Democratic Governors. There was also no statistically significant difference between Red and Blue states. Thus, the null hypothesis that the means are same by partisanship of Governors or people in the states was accepted at the .05 level of significance.

Conclusion:¶

Though the response to this pandemic appears to be structured by partisanship, covid cases per capita does not seem to be influenced by the polarity, at least at the moment. In spite of this, there is undoubtedly a need to present a united front and forge a consensus to fight this virus. There are limitations to this study. Firstly, we only have considered covid cases per 100k and not taken any other parameter for arriving at this conclusion. Secondly, there are possibilities of increased number of cases in certain states because of the neighboring states. Future studies could focus on other indicators like Rate of Positive Tests and study further on how people's response, to Social Distancing measures and other public health guidelines, vary based on the partisanship of states.
